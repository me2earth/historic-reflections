The Itinerary, day 4

After a too quick dash through additional fascinating streets and small piazzas we arrived at Piazza di Santa Croce, a magnificent civic space dominated visually by the Church of Santa Croce, but socially by the profusion of shops, restaurants and public space.

The church is a marvel of flamboyant decorative detail on the main facade (little effort was wasted on the sides, one of which was crammed right across a street, that was more of an alley, from tiny storefronts and the back doors of residences). To our American eyes, it is strangely colorful.

The interior of the church was more restrained than later renaissance efforts that we were to see in Rome. It was particularly interesting because of the many tombs of famous renaissance luminaries: Galileo, Machiavelli, Michelangelo, and so on. Each had a section of wall in which an elaborate sculpture was installed, paid for by patrons and the estates of the deceased, thus funding the church's activities, and enriching the overpaid clergy (vow of poverty, indeed).
Florence
Galileo's tomb, installed much later as amends for the church's mistreatment of him, features his bust plus two allegorical figures, typifying the traditional approach for all the tombs there but one. Michelangelo's alone features three allegorical figures signifying his rare mastery of the three distinct disciplines of painting, sculpture and architecture.
