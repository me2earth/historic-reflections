Mark Elster

Grasse, and St. Paul de Vence

The Itinerary, day 3 part 2

After what already feels like a full day, we reluctantly pile into the bus for the long journey back to Monaco, and the second half of this adventurous excursion…
Grasse and St. Paul de Vence
NancyEllen loved the sales clerk’s advice, "Scent placed behind the ear is for your lover; on pulse points, for everyone else; on your chest, just for you." [Ed.: I might disagree with that last one.]
Another short hop deposited us in a delightful hilltop village called St. Paul de Vence. The perfect exemplar of a well preserved medieval walled town. Except for modern wares and associated signage, and a few other amenities, it is as if development was arrested 500–600 years ago.

Impossibly narrow passages, stone walls and cobbled streets transport the visitor to another time. A time that is outright illegal in America! More and more, as we visit various sites, I am struck at how delightful they are, and how forbidden these architectural treats are to everyday America— forbidden to us at home by the well–meaning but darkly oppressive overlords of the administrative state who have decided to take care of us and prevent all manner of imagined and real harms—at the expense of variety, wonder, and imagination. It is abundantly clear, in contrast with this environment, that it is not worth the price.

The problem for urban planners everywhere is denialism of the unpredictable (and therefore un–plan–able) wonders of emergent order. The best environments aren’t planned, they emerge from the myriad decisions of individuals. Medieval and Renaissance Europe is a testament to this reality so central to human nature. It is too bad that we deny its full flower in our country (I also blame the lobbyists, lawyers and the unscrupulous greed and morals of those that hire them to extract unearned wealth or to impose their will on others—we’re looking at you, corporatists, environmentalists and so–called social justice warriors).
The famous Grasse perfume factory is centered in a region known for its lavender and other fragrant flowers. Traditionally, hand–picked petals were laid on screens layered with lard which would slowly, over the course of months, absorb the essential oils and fragrance of the flowers, to be later distilled, much like liquor. Now modern chemical processes are used, but other ancient methods remain, such as the "Nose," who designs each fragrance. Years of intense study and training (in one of the three "Schools of Noses") instills an ability to discern thousands of scents most of us cannot differentiate. Or maybe, as with wine sommeliers, some of this is just imagined. In any case, a stop at the gift shop was also pleasantly educational, not to mention wallet–lightening! Even us guys learned a thing or two.
We walked the tiny town from end–to–end and stem–to–stern several times, stopping to enjoy simple sandwiches and ice cream. The shops didn’t do too much damage, sending us off with some linens, a silver heart, some honey and rose jam (that spoiled literally days after opening because of their adherence to principals of organic nonsense).

We began to get alarmed that there were no bathrooms in this place when we uncovered a pay toilet that proved to be a technological marvel with a mild intimidation factor. Since it only took exact change and we hadn’t been using cash, the first trick was coming up with payment. Once inside, it is apparent the whole dank, cold and dripping room is sprayed with water and disinfectant between uses. Unfortunately it is not clear what triggers this action, so the unfamiliar user anxiously sits on the ice cold still wet stainless steel seat contemplating if they will make it out of this place dry, and agonizing over whether it is such a good idea to press the flush button! I picture that most users prepare for exit by edging as close to the door as possible, stretching out to trigger the button whilst leaping out of the target zone all in one motion (at least that was my approach).
This will sound as a discordant contradiction to my earlier bemoaning of America’s self–imposed deprivation of the joys of emergent order and the resultant dividends of delightful architecture and urban environments, but hear me out. A visit to St. Paul de Vence seems oddly similar to a visit to a zoo, or natural history museum. There is a voyeuristic allure to peeking in on the lives of a people who forego the modern expectations of privacy, space and mobility in a self–inflicted frozen state of deprivation and subservience to an imagined ascetic ideal (that is historically non–existent), compensated by an almost Disneyesque make–believe life as a simple medieval village. A land whom time has forgotten. One whose existence is predicated on the productivity and excess wealth produced by those who cannot realistically live in such a commercially produced fictional place. Essentially, they live as actors in a production sustained by patrons here to see the show (all that is missing are period costumes and traveling minstrels). The hidden irony: changing almost anything here is illegal in a similarly oppressive way as America’s system of Landuse controls. Fortunately, the people who live there self–select — they clearly enjoy the other rich rewards of their environment and preserve it for those of us who enjoy a visit but would find the place too stifling to call home.

Cynical observations aside, St. Paul de Vence really is impossibly quaint. Every corner turns up a new visual wonder. To the observant eye, even such mundain affairs as doors, windows, passageways and storm water control engender delight.
Turning inland to the foothills of the Alps we wind through several ordinary towns inevitably layered in the rich history of conquest by the Romans, Greeks, Tuscans and others; past vineyards (some of them saved by California vines after disease struck vulnerable monocultures that had nearly decimated French viticulture) and into the flower–growing region of the Parfumeraries.
